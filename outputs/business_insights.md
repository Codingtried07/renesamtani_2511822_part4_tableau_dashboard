# Business Insights

Eight insights drawn from the Executive Dashboard. Each one notes what the chart shows, the supporting numbers, what it means for the business, and what to do next.

---

## 1. Sales trend is volatile month-to-month but trending up

**Observation**: Monthly sales swing between roughly ₹5M and ₹11M across the 24-month window, with no clear seasonal pattern but a gentle upward drift toward late 2025.

**Evidence**: Sales Trend chart shows the lowest month at around ₹5.3M (mid-2024) and the highest at around ₹10.8M (late 2025). Profit tracks sales closely, suggesting margin stays roughly stable as volume changes.

**Interpretation**: The business is not seasonal in any obvious way, but the noise between months is high enough that single-month performance is a poor indicator. A rolling 3-month average would be more useful for decision-making than month-on-month comparisons.

**Recommended action**: Switch monthly performance reviews to use a 3-month rolling average. Investigate the mid-2024 dip and the late-2025 peak to understand whether either was driven by campaigns, pricing changes, or external events.

---

## 2. South region leads but the four regions are tightly clustered

**Observation**: South generates the most sales at ₹64.7M but the gap between regions is smaller than expected. East is second at ₹52M and West is the smallest at ₹49M. Profit margins are also close, ranging from 15.1% to 15.6%.

**Evidence**: Regional Performance chart shows South at 15.4% margin, East at 15.6%, North at 15.2%, West at 15.1%.

**Interpretation**: There is no underperforming region to worry about and no standout region to scale up. Regional strategy is probably not the right lever for growth right now. The fact that East has the highest margin despite middling sales suggests it may be a more efficient region to invest in next.

**Recommended action**: Run a deeper analysis on East to identify why margins are highest there. If reasons are repeatable (better category mix, less discounting, lower returns), apply those patterns to other regions.

---

## 3. Technology drives almost all of the profit, Furniture barely breaks even

**Observation**: Technology sub-categories generate the four highest profit bars (Copiers, Accessories, Phones, Machines all around ₹6.5M to ₹7.3M each). Furniture's profit is suppressed by significant losses in Tables and Bookcases.

**Evidence**: Category Profitability shows Technology contributing roughly 84% of total profit on 71% of sales. Furniture sits at only 6.9% margin overall, with Tables and Bookcases showing visible red loss segments crossing the zero reference line.

**Interpretation**: The business is effectively a technology retailer that also sells furniture and office supplies. Furniture is dragging the overall margin down. Either Furniture needs a pricing rework, supplier renegotiation, or scope reduction (drop the worst-performing sub-categories like Bookcases).

**Recommended action**: Review Furniture sourcing costs and pricing. Consider discontinuing or restructuring Bookcases specifically. Look at whether Furniture is acting as a loss-leader to attract Technology buyers, and if so, quantify that effect before cutting it.

---

## 4. Home Office is the most profitable customer segment

**Observation**: All three customer segments deliver similar total sales (around ₹70M to ₹74M), but Home Office produces the highest profit margin at 15.51%, ahead of Consumer at 15.34% and Corporate at 15.18%.

**Evidence**: Customer Segment chart shows Home Office on top for both sales and profit, with the highest margin label.

**Interpretation**: The margin gap is small but consistent. Home Office buyers are slightly less price-sensitive or buying a more profitable product mix. Worth understanding what drives this since the segment is also the smallest by volume.

**Recommended action**: Profile Home Office orders by category mix and discount usage to understand what makes them more profitable. Test whether Consumer and Corporate behaviour can be nudged toward the same patterns (e.g. less aggressive discounting on Corporate accounts).

---

## 5. Discounts above 20% are where margins fall apart

**Observation**: The Discount vs Profit scatter shows a clean break around the 20% threshold. Below 20%, profit ranges from break-even to ₹130K+ per order. Above 20%, the cluster shifts down, with negative-profit orders appearing visibly at 25%, 30%, and 35% discount tiers.

**Evidence**: 30%+ discount orders average a loss of ₹1,601 per order. The scatter shows ~20 visibly negative dots concentrated at discount levels of 0.25 and above, mostly from Furniture (blue dots in the loss zone).

**Interpretation**: Discounting is destroying margin past a fairly clear threshold. Either the discount tiers are being applied to products that cannot absorb them, or sales teams are using discounts as a shortcut to close deals on goods that should be sold at higher prices.

**Recommended action**: Cap automatic discounts at 20% without explicit approval. Audit the orders showing losses at 30%+ discount to identify which categories and customers are driving the pattern. Review whether sales incentives reward closing volume regardless of margin.

---

## 6. Shipping delays concentrate in Standard Class

**Observation**: Most orders are in the Fast (2-3 days) and Standard (4-5 days) buckets. Almost every order in the Delayed (6-9 days) bucket is shipped via Standard Class. Same Day and First Class do not appear in the Delayed bucket at all.

**Evidence**: Shipping Performance chart shows the Delayed bucket at around 670 orders, of which the vast majority show as the Standard Class colour. Premium ship modes (Same Day, First Class) are visible in faster buckets but absent from Delayed.

**Interpretation**: The premium shipping options are fulfilling their service-level commitments. The bottleneck is Standard Class, which makes up the bulk of orders and also accounts for nearly all the delays. This is an operational efficiency problem in the cheapest shipping tier.

**Recommended action**: Investigate Standard Class delivery operations. Identify whether delays are concentrated in specific regions, specific carriers, or specific product types. If a subset of Standard orders can be moved to First Class at low cost, that may improve customer experience without significant logistics spend.

---

## 7. Furniture is the source of nearly all returns

**Observation**: Bookcases (8.42%), Furnishings (around 8%), and Chairs (around 7.5%) all return at rates well above the overall 4.55% average. The lowest return rates are in Technology (Copiers at 2.72%).

**Evidence**: Return Analysis shows three Furniture sub-categories sitting in the red zone of the diverging colour scale, all above the average reference line. Office Supplies and Technology sub-categories sit in the green band, mostly below average.

**Interpretation**: Furniture has both a profit problem and a return problem, and the two are probably connected. High return rates suggest product quality issues, damage during shipping, or buyer expectations not matching reality. Each return also erodes the already thin Furniture margin.

**Recommended action**: Investigate Furniture returns by reason code (if available) and look at the customer rating data on the same orders. If reviews mention damage or quality, the issue is upstream. If reviews mention mismatched expectations, the product pages need clearer photography and dimensions.

---

## 8. The biggest risk and biggest opportunity sit in the same place

**Observation**: Furniture combines the lowest margin (6.9%), the highest return rate (7.67%), and the largest concentration of discount-driven losses (Bookcases and Tables show negative profit dots in the scatter). Technology by contrast has the strongest margin and the lowest return rate.

**Evidence**: Cross-referencing Category Profitability (Furniture's red loss segments), Return Analysis (Furniture in red), and Discount vs Profit (Furniture blue dots below zero at high discounts) shows the same category is the weakest performer in three different views.

**Interpretation**: Furniture is the single biggest risk on the dashboard and the most obvious opportunity. The risk is that it continues to drag down overall profitability and return rates. The opportunity is that even modest improvements in Furniture (better pricing, fewer returns, capped discounts) would move the headline numbers visibly.

**Recommended action**: Treat Furniture as a strategic review priority. The work should cover: pricing audit, supplier quality review, discount policy, and a serious look at whether sub-categories like Bookcases should remain in the catalogue at all. A 3-month focused effort on Furniture is likely to produce more measurable impact than spreading the same effort across the whole business.
